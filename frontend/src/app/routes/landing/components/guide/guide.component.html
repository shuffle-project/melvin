Why Didactic Recommendations?

Digitisation in education must not become an end in itself. The aim of concepts for digital teaching and learning should be to enhance the quality of teaching, improve its effectiveness, and at the same time promote equal opportunities within the higher education system.

So far, however, the topics of equal opportunities, inclusion, and digitalisation have rarely been considered in conjunction – especially not from a subject-didactic perspective. While digital tools offer a wide range of possible applications, not everything that is technically feasible is also didactically meaningful or accessible.

Melvin, a video platform for teaching, is also first and foremost a tool that offers various functions and creative possibilities. However, what really matters is how these possibilities are implemented from a didactic point of view. Through careful planning and thoughtful use, it is possible to ensure accessible and equitable access to content for all users.

The didactic guidelines provided here are intended to support teaching staff in using Melvin in ways that are both inclusive and didactically effective.

Planning the Video

Before starting video production, careful planning from a didactic, content-related, and technical perspective is essential. This ensures that the video meets the intended learning objectives and reaches all students effectively.

Didactic Planning

General Instructional Framework
It is crucial to determine in advance how the videos will be integrated into teaching and for what purpose they will be used. What role do the videos play within the broader instructional framework? Defining this objective helps in designing the videos effectively.
Self-Regulated Learning
Providing videos places more responsibility on students for their own learning. Learning videos should therefore foster strategies for self-regulated learning (SRL) and motivate students to engage actively with the content.
Optimal Video Length
Depending on the purpose, learning videos can vary in length, but they should not exceed a certain duration to avoid cognitive overload. Empirical research suggests a maximum duration of 20 minutes.
Structuring the Video
The longer the video, the more important it is to maintain a clear structure and include pauses to support comprehension. Planning recognizable sections (introduction, main part, conclusion) in advance is advisable. Creating a script, storyboard, or screenplay can help structure the content and voiceover effectively.
Flipped Classroom
When videos are used as part of an Flipped Classroom model, clear incentives should be provided for students to prepare for deeper engagement during in-person sessions. If a video is assigned to a specific session, this should be clearly communicated, and students should have sufficient time to review the content and any accompanying materials.
Microlearning Units
If a video is intended to introduce or deepen a specific aspect, a short and focused microlearning unit (also known as a "learning nugget") may be appropriate. These concise videos effectively present knowledge in just a few minutes. Multiple microlearning units can be organized into a series, with clear titles and sequencing information to enhance clarity.
Examples:
  • A video on how to effectively search for academic articles in databases.
  • A video explaining proper scientific citation methods.
Long-Term Usability
Is the video intended for one-time use, or should it be accessible across multiple semesters or courses? For long-term use, prioritize general and broadly applicable information while minimizing temporary references (e.g., links to potentially outdated websites).
Example: A video explaining the fundamentals of qualitative or quantitative research methods in social sciences.
Ensuring Accessibility – Universal Design for Learning (UDL)/Multisensory Design Principle
To promote accessibility, information should be presented in multiple formats—for example, through both auditory and visual means. Tools like Melvin, which automatically generate subtitles and transcripts, can facilitate this approach.
Verbalizing Visual Elements
All essential visual elements used in the video should also be described verbally. Ideally, the key content of an image or figure should be integrated into the spoken text. If this is not feasible, a separate audio or text-based description should be provided as an additional resource.
Accessible Digital Materials
When combining videos with other digital learning formats such as text, images, or interactive tools, ensure that all materials comply with accessibility standards. See document Tips for Accessible Tools

Content Planning

Clear Topic Focus
Each video should address a well-defined topic, clearly stated at the beginning. A precise title enhances recognizability and helps students navigate the content effectively.
Defining Learning Objectives
Each video should specify its learning objectives at the beginning and in the course syllabus. This improves transparency and aids students in organizing their knowledge acquisition.
Exam-Relevant Content
If a video covers content relevant to exams, this should be explicitly stated in the learning objectives. Additionally, providing practice questions can help students gain confidence in exam situations.
Encouraging Active Participation
Integrating interactive elements, such as exercises, can promote active learning and deeper engagement with the material.
Reviewing Acquired Knowledge
Where can students test their newly acquired knowledge? Quizzes or targeted exercises can be embedded in the video or included in follow-up in-person sessions.
Clarifying Technical Terms
Complex concepts or technical terminology should be clearly explained, with references to additional resources (e.g., central websites or literature) when necessary.
Providing Feedback and Reflection Opportunities
Videos can include reflection questions or closing exercises to reinforce learning. Opportunities for feedback and discussion can be facilitated through digital learning platforms or in subsequent in-person sessions.

Technical Planning

Ensuring High-Quality Audio and Video
Both video and sound quality should be optimal, with good lighting and a distraction-free recording environment. A calm and clear speaking style, with sufficient pauses, enhances comprehensibility. A neutral background helps keep the focus on the content.
Technical Equipment Considerations
Reliable technical equipment and pre-recording checks ensure smoother production. Test recordings can help identify optimal settings. Depending on the setup, using two monitors may facilitate the recording process.
Clear and Informative File Naming
The video file name should be meaningful and include key information such as title, author, topic, and date.
Example: Hearing_Disorders_Baumgart_Pediatric_Audiology_WS23_24
Easy Video Access
Learning videos should be easily accessible on the learning platform. An accompanying syllabus summarizing the video’s content in bullet points enhances clarity and transparency.
